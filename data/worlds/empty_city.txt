The underpass runs below the ring road for forty metres. Graffiti covers both walls at shoulder height. A car sits abandoned across both lanes near the far end, all four doors open. The underpass lights are on, half the tubes dead. On the hood of the car something has been arranged: wire bent into limbs, a child's shoe for a head, glass shards pressed into the chest in a pattern that is not random. The underpass smells of standing water and old rubber.

The service road follows the embankment alongside the motorway. A crash barrier separates the service road from the live lanes. A pallet of bagged cement sits on the verge, the bags split, the whole mass set solid. A traffic cone stands on top of the cement, upright, as if placed deliberately. The service road surface is cracked and weeds push through every joint.

The retail arcade connects the underpass to the central plaza. The escalators are stopped. The roll-down gates on most units are closed, some tagged, some plain. A ticket machine blinks a fault code against the tiled wall of the arcade. The arcade floor is vinyl, cracked along the centre seam. A food court occupies the back half of the arcade, the chairs still pushed under the tables.

The central plaza opens between four office towers. A fountain sits at the centre of the plaza, dry, the basin holding a shallow pool of rainwater and one shopping trolley. An effigy stands at the rim of the fountain basin: lengths of conduit bent into legs, wire twisted into a torso, something that was once a coat draped over the wire shoulders. The effigy faces the fountain. The plaza paving slabs are lifted in places where roots have worked under them.

The maintenance tunnel runs beneath the arcade and the plaza. Access is through a steel door set into the wall below the escalators. The maintenance tunnel is low, the ceiling crossed with pipes and cable trays. A steel hatch sits flush in the floor midway along the maintenance tunnel, approximately one metre square. The gap around the hatch is wide enough to slip a cable tie through. A cable tie has been looped around a pipe below the hatch and knotted, holding the hatch from the underside.

The office lobby of the eastern tower has a reception desk facing the entrance. The screen on the desk is cracked but still lit. The lift doors stand open. A fire door at the back of the office lobby leads to a stairwell. The office lobby floor is polished concrete, still clean except for a single set of footprints in fine grey dust leading from the entrance to the lift and stopping there.

The stairwell climbs the eastern tower from the ground floor to the roof. The stairwell walls are painted breeze block, the paint peeling in long strips. Emergency lighting is on at every landing. The stairwell smells of damp plaster. On the fourth floor landing of the stairwell someone has assembled an effigy against the wall: a broomstick for a spine, cable ties holding plastic bottles to the stick at shoulder height, a hard hat balanced on top. The effigy leans slightly forward, as if looking down the stairwell.

The car park occupies the basement and the first two levels below the plaza. The car park is still half full. The cars have been parked normally and locked, as if their owners intended to return. The car park lighting runs on a motion sensor that activates when anything moves through the car park. The car park smells of oil and concrete dust. In the far corner of the second basement level of the car park, close to the wall, three effigies stand in a rough circle facing inward. Each effigy is made from different materials: wire and glass, plastic bags and duct tape, office chairs with coats draped over the backs.

The railway platform sits below ground level, accessible by escalator from the transit hall. The railway platform is lit by strips of fluorescent light mounted low on the walls. The departure board still cycles through arrivals on a loop but the times are all in the past. The railway platform benches are fixed to the floor, the seats intact. A single effigy stands at the far end of the railway platform near the mouth of the tunnel: thin wire bound with electrical tape, taller than a person, its proportions slightly wrong.

The transit hall connects the railway platform to the street level. The transit hall ceiling is high, the walls tiled in a pattern of overlapping rectangles. Turnstiles block the exits from the transit hall, most of them jammed open. A row of ticket machines lines the wall of the transit hall, all showing the same fault screen. Dried leaves have blown in through the entrance and gathered in the corners of the transit hall.

The motorway overpass carries six lanes above the district. The motorway overpass is supported by concrete columns set at regular intervals through the district below. The underside of the motorway overpass is low enough to touch from the top of a van. Pigeons roost on the ledges of the motorway overpass. The noise of wind through the motorway overpass is constant and directional. On three of the concrete columns of the motorway overpass, effigies have been fixed at head height using cable ties: small, rough, assembled from zip ties and broken plastic, none of them identical.

The service tunnel runs parallel to the main service road at a lower level. The service tunnel carries conduit, water pipes, and cable trays along both walls. The service tunnel is lit by bare bulbs on a string, most of them working. The service tunnel floor is concrete with a shallow drainage channel running down the centre. The service tunnel connects the eastern plant room to the western plant room without passing through any public space.

The eastern plant room houses the electrical switchgear and water pumps for the eastern tower and the arcade. The eastern plant room is warm, the switchgear still running on some form of standby power. A row of meters on the wall of the eastern plant room shows readings that are still changing. In the corner of the eastern plant room, someone has placed three objects in a line on the floor: a work glove, a glass bottle, and a single cable tie, arranged with care.

The western plant room is cooler and smells of damp. The western plant room contains a boiler that is no longer running, the pilot light out. Shelves on the walls of the western plant room hold tools, spare fittings, and labelled boxes going back many years. The most recent label on any box in the western plant room reads four years ago. A door at the back of the western plant room leads to the loading bay.

The loading bay faces the service road at the rear of the retail block. The loading bay is a concrete apron with dock levellers set into the wall. Two delivery vehicles are parked in the loading bay, one with its rear doors open, empty. The loading bay is sheltered by a canopy above the dock doors. Under the canopy of the loading bay, just inside the shadow line, an effigy has been assembled from pallet wrap and broken wooden pallets. The effigy in the loading bay is the largest in the district. Its arms are raised above its head.

The rooftop of the eastern tower is accessible from the stairwell. The rooftop is flat, with gravel ballast and several plant units still running on standby. A communications mast at the edge of the rooftop carries a red warning lamp that still blinks at regular intervals. The view from the rooftop takes in the full extent of the district and the motorway overpass to the west. Looking down from the rooftop into the central plaza, the effigy at the fountain is not visible from directly above.

The pedestrian bridge crosses the canal between the retail block and the apartment towers. The pedestrian bridge is steel and glass, enclosed. The glass panels of the pedestrian bridge are intact except for one at the midpoint, which has been replaced with plywood. The pedestrian bridge affords a view of the canal and the motorway overpass to the north. At the midpoint of the pedestrian bridge, where the plywood panel is, an effigy has been seated on the floor with its back against the plywood, legs extended, arms resting on its knees. The effigy is made from a coat stuffed with newspaper and cable-tied at the wrists and ankles.

The canal towpath runs below the pedestrian bridge along the south bank of the canal. The canal towpath is tarmac, cracked, with bollards at intervals. The canal itself is slow and carries debris against the south bank. The water in the canal is brown-grey. A narrowboat is moored permanently near the pedestrian bridge, its ropes rotted but still holding. The narrowboat windows are covered from the inside with foil.

The apartment tower lobby opens onto the pedestrian bridge at first floor level. The apartment tower lobby has a post room to one side, the pigeonholes still full of uncollected mail. The lift in the apartment tower lobby is stuck between floors and cannot be called. A noticeboard in the apartment tower lobby still carries notices with dates from the period before. The apartment tower lobby floor is carpeted, the carpet worn in a path from the door to the lift.

The upper floors of the apartment tower are accessible only by the stairwell. The apartment tower stairwell is identical to the eastern tower stairwell but quieter. At the twelfth floor landing of the apartment tower stairwell, the emergency lighting is off and the only light comes from the window slit in the landing door. Someone has left a torch on the landing floor, switched off, with a note attached to it that reads only a number: 12.

The roof of the apartment tower is not accessible from the stairwell. The door to the roof of the apartment tower is bolted from the outside.

The district perimeter is marked by a concrete wall three metres high running along the north side of the district. The concrete wall is topped with wire that has mostly corroded and lies flat against the top of the wall. Gates in the concrete wall are padlocked. Through the gates in the concrete wall, a road leads north into a part of the city that is identical in structure to this district: the same tower blocks, the same retail arcade plan, the same motorway overpass. The road north extends without visible end.

The district extends further than any single route through it suggests. Side streets between the towers lead into further precincts of the same character. The further precincts have the same infrastructure, the same stopped escalators, the same fault-code ticket machines. The effigies in the further precincts are built from the same materials but assembled with increasing care. The further precincts eventually reach another motorway overpass, another canal, another retail arcade. The city does not end.
